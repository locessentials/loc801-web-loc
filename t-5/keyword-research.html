TOPIC 5

Keyword Research & SEO Metrics

Learn how to conduct effective keyword research and interpret SEO metrics to optimize your multilingual content for search engine visibility.

üîç What is Keyword Research?

Keyword research is the process of identifying the specific words and phrases your target audience uses when searching for information, products, or services related to your content. It's a foundational SEO practice that helps you understand what people are looking for and how to make your content discoverable.

The keyword research process involves three main steps: selecting seed words (broad terms related to your topic), generating ideas (expanding those terms into more specific variations), and evaluating options (analyzing which keywords offer the best opportunities for your content).

üìå The Status Quo of Web Localization

While website localization often includes a language picker with content available in multiple languages, this approach frequently falls short. As discussed in my article "The Problem with Website Language Pickers," content is rarely maintained equally across languages, users can compare experiences, and keyword optimization doesn't translate one-to-one. It's often better to create market-specific sites that respond directly to each audience's search behaviors.

üìä Key SEO Metrics

Understanding the following metrics will help you evaluate which keywords are worth targeting for your content. These metrics guide you toward opportunities where you can realistically rank well and reach your intended audience.

Search Intent

The underlying reason someone performs a search. Are they trying to buy something (commercial intent), learn about a topic (informational intent), find a specific website (navigational intent), or complete an action (transactional intent)? Your content should match the searcher's intent to rank well.

Monthly Search Volume

The average number of times a keyword is searched per month. Higher volume means more potential traffic, but also typically more competition. A keyword with 10,000 monthly searches might be harder to rank for than one with 500 searches.

Search Volume Trend

How search volume changes over time. Some keywords are seasonal (like "holiday gifts"), others are growing in popularity, and some are declining. Understanding trends helps you create timely, relevant content.

Keyword SEO Difficulty

A score (typically 0-100) indicating how hard it would be to rank in the top 10 results for a keyword. Higher difficulty means more competition from established websites. New sites should focus on lower-difficulty keywords initially.

üéØ Types of Keywords

Keywords are categorized by their length and specificity. Understanding these categories helps you choose the right keywords for your content strategy.

Head Tail Keywords

Head tail keywords (also called "short-tail" or "fat head" keywords) are very broad, typically 1-2 words. Examples include "shoes," "translation," or "coffee." These keywords have extremely high search volume but also intense competition. They're difficult to rank for, and the search intent is often unclear‚Äîsomeone searching "shoes" could be looking to buy, research brands, or learn about shoe history.

Middle Tail Keywords

Middle tail keywords contain 2-3 words and are more specific. Examples include "running shoes," "medical translation," or "cold brew coffee." These offer a better balance between search volume and competition. The search intent becomes clearer, making it easier to create targeted content that satisfies the searcher's needs.

Long Tail Keywords

Long tail keywords are highly specific phrases of 4+ words, such as "best running shoes for flat feet," "certified medical translation services in Chicago," or "how to make cold brew coffee at home." While individual long-tail keywords have lower search volumes, they collectively represent the majority of web searches. They offer several advantages: less competition, clearer search intent, higher conversion rates, and easier ranking opportunities for newer websites.

‚ö° Strategy Tip

For new websites or entering new markets, focus on long-tail keywords first. As your site builds authority and you create more content, gradually target middle-tail and head-tail keywords. This approach gives you quick wins while building toward broader visibility.

KWFinder by Mangools

KWFinder is a user-friendly keyword research tool that's particularly good for beginners. It provides clear visualizations of keyword difficulty, search volume, and trends. The free tier allows limited searches per day, which is sufficient for learning purposes.

Getting Started with KWFinder

  1. Visit mangools.com/kwfinder and create a free account
  2. Enter your seed keyword in the search box
  3. Select your target location and language
  4. Review the results, paying attention to:
    • Keyword Difficulty (KD) - Aim for keywords with KD under 40 if your site is new
    • Search Volume - Look for keywords with at least 100-500 monthly searches
    • Trend - Check if interest is growing, stable, or declining
    • Search Intent - Ensure the search results match what your content offers
  5. Export promising keywords to a spreadsheet for content planning

When analyzing KWFinder results, look at the actual search results for your target keyword. Click on high-ranking pages to understand what type of content performs well. Are they blog posts, product pages, videos, or how-to guides? This competitive analysis helps you understand what you need to create to rank.

üìã Developing a Keyword Strategy

Effective keyword research isn't just about finding individual keywords‚Äîit's about developing a comprehensive strategy for your entire website. This requires tracking systems, content planning, and strategic gap filling.

Content-Keyword Alignment

Each page should be genuinely about the keyword you've assigned to it. The keyword should appear naturally in the page title, headings, first paragraph, and throughout the content. Don't force keywords where they don't fit‚ÄîGoogle's algorithms are sophisticated enough to detect artificial keyword stuffing, which can hurt your rankings.

Tracking Your Keywords

Create a spreadsheet or database to track which keywords you've targeted on which pages. This prevents accidental keyword cannibalization (multiple pages competing for the same keyword) and helps you see coverage gaps. Your tracking system should include the page URL, primary keyword, secondary keywords, current ranking position, and content status.

Filling Keyword Gaps

Once you've created content for your initial keyword list, analyze what topics your site doesn't yet cover. Look for related keywords that your competitors rank for but you don't. These gaps represent opportunities to expand your content and capture additional search traffic. Prioritize gaps that align with your business goals and where you have genuine expertise to share.

üåê Multilingual Strategy

When managing multilingual sites, maintain separate keyword strategies for each language and market. Don't simply translate your English keywords‚Äîresearch what terms your target audience actually uses in their language. Cultural differences, regional variations, and local search behaviors mean that effective keywords rarely translate directly.
