Traditional SEO

Learn the fundamentals of Search Engine Optimization (SEO) and why understanding how search engines work is essential for creating discoverable multilingual content.

üîç What is Traditional SEO?

Traditional SEO (Search Engine Optimization) is the practice of improving websites to make them appear in high positions in search engine results pages (SERPs). When someone searches for information on Google, Bing, or other search engines, SEO helps determine which websites appear at the top of those results.

For localization professionals, understanding SEO is crucial because translated content needs to be discoverable by your target audience. A perfectly localized website serves little purpose if search engines cannot find it or if it ranks poorly in search results. Good SEO practices ensure that your multilingual content reaches the people who are searching for it.

Traditional SEO focuses on optimizing content for search engines like Google, which use complex algorithms to crawl, index, and rank web pages. This differs from newer approaches like LLM discoverability (which we'll cover later), but the foundational principles remain essential for any web presence.

üï∑Ô∏è How Search Engines Work

Understanding how search engines work is fundamental to creating content that can be found. Search engines don't magically know about every website‚Äîthey follow a systematic process to discover, understand, and rank content.

Search Engine Market Share

While Google dominates globally with approximately 90% market share, different regions favor different search engines. In the United States and Canada, about 6% use Bing and 4% use Yahoo. In Russia, Yandex commands 45% of the market. In China, Baidu leads with 65% market share. Privacy-oriented engines like DuckDuckGo serve niche audiences who prioritize data protection.

For localization work, this means you need to research which search engines your target audience actually uses. While we focus primarily on Google in this course (as the market leader), optimizing for regional search engines may be necessary depending on your markets.

The Three Components of Search Engines

Search engines consist of three main parts:

1. Crawlers (also called spiders or bots)

Automated programs that travel the web 24/7, following links from page to page. Think of them as explorers mapping the internet, collecting content like text, images, videos, and news items. They save the HTML version of each page they visit.

2. The Index

A massive database where search engines store information about every page they've crawled. This index is updated whenever the crawler revisits a site and finds new or changed content. How often Google's crawler visits your site depends on how important Google deems your site and how frequently you update it.

3. The Algorithm

The complex system that takes data from the index and calculates which pages best match a user's search query. The algorithm considers hundreds of factors to determine the ranking order shown on search engine results pages (SERPs). While the exact algorithm is secret, extensive testing and Google's own communications help us understand important ranking factors.

How Search Engines Find Your Site

For a search engine to discover your website, there must first be a link to your site from another website that the search engine already knows. Following that link leads to the first crawler session and the first save in the index. Think of indexed sites as a network‚Äîcrawlers need a road (link) to reach new sites.

This is why links are so important for SEO. Both internal links (from one part of your website to another) and external links (from other websites to yours) have tremendous effects on search engine rankings. The effect can be positive or negative depending on whether the links make sense contextually.

The Importance of Links

Search engines use links to determine how important a page is. Pages with more links pointing to them are generally considered more important. However, not all links are equal‚Äîlinks from websites that themselves have many external links are generally more valuable than links from small websites with few external links.

Localization Insight: When creating multilingual sites, proper linking between language versions and maintaining link value across languages becomes a technical SEO consideration. When creating multilingual sites, proper linking between language versions and maintaining link value across languages becomes a technical SEO consideration. This involves implementing hreflang tags to tell search engines which language versions of a page exist, and ensuring that internal links preserve SEO authority when users switch between languages.

üåê Holistic SEO: A Comprehensive Approach

Holistic SEO means looking at the bigger picture rather than focusing on isolated tactics. It's about creating the best possible website for your users, knowing that search engines want to show users the best results.

On-Page vs. Off-Page Factors

SEO factors can be divided into two categories:

On-Page Factors

Elements on your own website that you can control, including site structure, content quality, page speed, technical code quality, keyword usage, and mobile optimization.

Off-Page Factors

Elements outside your website that influence your ranking, such as links from other websites, social media attention, brand mentions, and other marketing activities.

Google's Mission and Your Strategy

Google's mission is to "organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful." To achieve this, Google must show users the best possible results for their searches‚Äîotherwise people would stop using Google.

This means that if your website genuinely is the best option for users searching for your topic, Google wants to rank it highly. Your goal should be to build and maintain the best possible website. Ranking in Google will improve over time if your website is of extremely high quality and truly serves user needs.

Key Elements of Holistic SEO

A holistic approach to SEO means focusing on all these aspects:

  • Proper Keyword Research: Understanding what words and queries your target audience actually uses when searching for your content or products
  • Good Technical SEO: Ensuring fast page load times, fixing broken links (404 errors), maintaining clean code, and making your site easy for both users and search engines to navigate
  • Great User Experience: Creating intuitive navigation, clear content hierarchy, accessible design, and removing friction from user interactions
  • Quality Content: Writing original, useful content that answers user questions and provides genuine value‚Äînot just content written to manipulate search rankings
  • Social Media Strategy: Building awareness and engagement on platforms where your audience spends time, which can drive traffic and signal content quality
  • Mobile Optimization: Ensuring excellent experiences on mobile devices, where most web browsing now occurs
  • Security: Using HTTPS, protecting user data, and demonstrating trustworthiness through secure practices
  • Accessibility: Making your content usable by everyone, including people using screen readers, keyboard navigation, or other assistive technologies

Why Holistic SEO Works

Websites that excel in all these areas typically see:

  • Lower Bounce Rates: When people find what they're looking for quickly, they stay on your site longer and explore more pages
  • Higher Social Media Attention: Quality content gets shared, which signals to search engines that your content is valuable
  • Better Conversion Rates: Users who have a good experience are more likely to complete desired actions (purchasing, subscribing, contacting you)
  • Sustainable Rankings: Sites built on solid fundamentals maintain rankings even as algorithms change

What NOT to Do: Short-Term Tricks

Some tactics try to exploit loopholes in search algorithms to rank higher than deserved. These include:

‚ö†Ô∏è Keyword Stuffing

Filling pages with keywords or numbers that appear out of context, often in lists or groups. Some sites even use white text to hide keywords from users while still exposing them to search engines. This violates search engine guidelines.

‚ö†Ô∏è Buying Links

Paying for links or exchanging free products for links to artificially boost rankings. Search engines have become sophisticated at detecting unnatural link patterns and will penalize sites that engage in these practices.

‚ö†Ô∏è Page Swapping

Getting a page ranked for one topic, then replacing the content with something completely different after achieving good rankings. This deceptive practice will eventually be detected and penalized.

Google's algorithm updates like Panda (2011) and Penguin (2012) specifically targeted these manipulative tactics. Sites using such tricks were heavily penalized and lost their rankings. As algorithms become more sophisticated, short-term tactics become increasingly ineffective and risky.

The Sustainable Approach: Focus on creating genuinely helpful content and excellent user experiences. This strategy works today and will continue working as search algorithms improve. Think long-term sustainability rather than short-term gains.

üì± The Critical Importance of Mobile Experience

Over 50% of web traffic now comes from mobile devices‚Äîand in many markets, mobile usage exceeds desktop usage by a significant margin. This means ensuring your website provides an excellent mobile experience is not optional; it's essential for both user satisfaction and search engine rankings.

Mobile-First Indexing

In 2018, Google switched to mobile-first indexing. This means Google primarily uses the mobile version of your site for indexing and ranking‚Äîeven when users are searching on desktop computers. If your site doesn't work well on mobile, your search rankings will suffer across all devices.

Testing Mobile Responsiveness

You can check how your site appears on different devices using the browser Developer Tools you learned about in Topic 3. Here's how:

Using Chrome DevTools for Mobile Testing

  1. Right-click anywhere on your webpage
  2. Click "Inspect" to open Developer Tools
  3. Click the "Toggle device toolbar" icon (it looks like a phone and tablet overlapping) or press Ctrl+Shift+M (Cmd+Shift+M on Mac)
  4. Use the dropdown at the top to select different device types (iPhone, iPad, various Android phones, tablets)
  5. Test how your content reflows, whether buttons are tappable, and if text remains readable

Responsive Design with CSS Media Queries

CSS media queries allow you to apply different styles based on device characteristics like screen width. This is how responsive design works‚Äîthe same HTML content styled differently for different screen sizes. 

/* Default styles for desktop */
.container {
    max-width: 1200px;
    padding: 20px;
}

/* Styles for tablets and smaller */
@media (max-width: 768px) {
    .container {
        padding: 15px;
    }
    
    .navigation {
        flex-direction: column;
    }
}

/* Styles for mobile phones */
@media (max-width: 420px) {
    .container {
        padding: 10px;
    }
    
    .text-content {
        font-size: 16px;
        line-height: 1.5;
    }
}

The @media property lets you define breakpoints where your layout changes. Common breakpoints include:

  • max-width: 420px - Mobile phones
  • max-width: 768px - Tablets and smaller devices
  • min-width: 1024px - Large desktop screens

Mobile responsive design becomes even more critical when working with multilingual sites where text expansion varies by language.

üìä Measuring SEO Performance

Understanding how users interact with your search results is essential for refining your SEO strategy. Key performance metrics help you evaluate whether your optimization efforts are attracting the right audience and meeting their needs.

1

Click-Through Rate (CTR)

CTR measures the percentage of people who click on your search result after seeing it. A higher CTR indicates that your title tags and meta descriptions are compelling and relevant to search queries. Industry benchmarks vary by position, but the top result typically achieves a CTR between 25-35%.

To improve CTR, craft descriptive titles that clearly communicate your page's value, include relevant keywords naturally, and write meta descriptions that answer the searcher's question or address their need.

2

Bounce Rate

Bounce rate represents the percentage of visitors who leave your site after viewing only one page. While not directly a ranking factor, high bounce rates may signal that your content doesn't match user intent or fails to engage visitors effectively.

However, context matters. A high bounce rate on a contact information page may be perfectly acceptable if users found what they needed. Conversely, a high bounce rate on a product page or article suggests users aren't finding the content valuable or relevant.

3

Dwell Time and Engagement

Dwell time refers to how long a user stays on your page after clicking through from search results. Longer dwell times typically indicate that users find your content valuable and engaging, which can positively influence rankings over time.

Improve dwell time by ensuring your content delivers on the promise made in your title and meta description, organizing information clearly with headers and sections, and providing comprehensive answers to user questions.

Tracking Your Metrics

Google Search Console provides CTR data for your search results, showing which queries generate clicks and which pages perform best. Google Analytics tracks bounce rate and engagement metrics, helping you identify content that resonates with visitors. Monitor these metrics regularly to identify trends and opportunities for improvement.
