üåê The GILT Framework

The GILT framework provides a comprehensive approach to creating products and services for international markets. Each component plays a distinct but interconnected role in global market success.

G - Globalization
g11n - Infrastructure Work

The infrastructure enterprises establish to carry out international operations, including legal frameworks, talent acquisition, and customer support across all markets.


I - Internationalization
i18n - Coding Work

Development of culturally neutral software with localization-ready architecture that can be customized to different locales during the localization process.


L - Localization
l10n - Design Work

Adaptation of products and services to specific locales, addressing both linguistic and non-textual elements like design, formatting, and cultural appropriateness.


T - Translation
t9n - Textual Work

The linguistic work of rendering content from one language to another. Translation involves both loss and addition of meaning inherent in cross-linguistic communication.

‚öôÔ∏è Software Internationalization Essentials

Proper internationalization prevents "i18n debt"‚Äîthe accumulation of issues that must be addressed before effective localization can occur. Key aspects include:

  • Externalizing strings by moving user-facing text out of code into separate resource files
  • Using Unicode encoding (UTF-8) to support multiple languages and character sets
  • Avoiding string concatenation with parameterized strings, as word order varies across languages
  • Allowing for text expansion in UI design‚Äîsome languages require significantly more space
  • Using locale-aware data types for dates, times, numbers, and currency
  • Supporting bidirectional text for languages that read right-to-left
  • Implementing proper plural rules‚Äîlanguages vary significantly in plural forms
  • Separating text from images to enable translation without redesign

üîÑ Localization Workflow

A guiding principle: "Time investments during front-end stages yield the greatest efficiencies on back-end processes." The localization workflow includes:

  • Work request submission and specifications definition
  • Quote approval and preproduction resource allocation
  • Source text pre-editing and terminology gathering
  • Pseudo-translation to identify text expansion/contraction needs
  • Machine and human translation - often performed in translation environment tools (TEnT)
  • Reintegration of translated content into product design
  • In-context and functionality quality checks
  • Final delivery to client

üìë Translation & Localization Specifications

Specifications transform the subjective nature of translation quality into objective, measurable requirements. According to ASTM International, specifications are "the explicit set of requirements to be satisfied by a material, product, system, or service."

Why Specifications Matter
When multiple translators translate the same content, no two will produce identical translations, and each will have their own opinions when performing quality evaluations. Specifications make implicit cultural expectations explicit and ensure all stakeholders‚Äîrequesters, end users, and production teams‚Äîhave aligned expectations.
