Professional Translator Selection Checklist

A structured checklist for evaluating translator qualifications when hiring for web localization projects‚Äîgrounded in ASTM F43 translation quality standards and adapted to the specific demands of the web.

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Identify the core proficiency areas used to evaluate professional translators
  • Apply web-localization-specific criteria when assessing translator qualifications
  • Distinguish between being bilingual and being a professional translator
  • Recognize the indicators that demonstrate competence in each proficiency area

üìã How to Use This Checklist

This checklist outlines the professional proficiency areas you should evaluate when selecting a translator for a web localization project. Each area includes specific indicators‚Äîconcrete signals that suggest a translator has the necessary expertise.

üí°
Not every indicator may be present. Use this checklist as a guide, not as a pass/fail test. A translator with deep subject matter expertise and extensive web localization experience may be a stronger candidate than one who checks more boxes on paper but lacks relevant specialization. Look for a pattern of competence across areas that matter most for your specific project.

The checklist is organized into seven proficiency areas. The first two‚Äîlanguage proficiency and translation expertise‚Äîare foundational requirements. The remaining areas help you assess fit for your specific project and organization.

1. Source and Target Language Proficiency

The translator must have strong proficiency in both the source language (the language of your original content) and the target language (the language into which it will be translated)‚Äîincluding the specific regional variety that your audience uses.

For example: English ‚Äì United States ‚Äì California; Spanish for Mexico City; Miami English or Spanish

üåê Web Localization Context
Regional language differences are especially visible online, and not adapting the content to linguistic conventions of the target region can result in users rejecting your content. For example, people in Mexico may feel offended if you only offer them translations written in Spanish for Spain. Style, idiomatic expressions, and mechanical features of language such as date formats and currency symbols vary significantly among the 20+ countries in which Spanish is spoken as a lingua franca. Make sure you specify the exact locale, so that your site contents can be written in exactly the language that your audience expects.

Indicators to Look For

  • Lived experience in a region where the target language is spoken natively
  • Professional work history conducted in the target language and locale
  • Education or degrees obtained in the target language or region
üîÑ

2. Translation Proficiency

Being bilingual does not make someone a professional translator. Translation is a distinct craft that requires training and practice. Look for evidence of professional translation work‚Äînot just language fluency.

For example: Experience translating marketing copy, technical documentation, or UI strings; editing or reviewing translations produced by others; working as a bilingual terminologist

üåê Web Localization Context
Web content presents unique translation challenges: character limits for UI elements, SEO keyword integration, writing for LLM discoverability, and the need to transcreate (or write from scratch) rather than translate blog posts and calls to action. Ask candidates if they have experience writing web-specific content.

Indicators to Look For

  • Professional translation experience (not just bilingualism)
  • Certificates in web-specific translation techniques or formal studies in translation or localization with related coursework
  • Demonstrated experience with web-specific content such as UI text, SEO metadata, marketing copy, or HTML-embedded strings
üéì

3. Subject Matter Specialization

Your website content exists within a subject domain‚Äîhealthcare, legal, e-commerce, software, travel, finance. A translator who specializes in that domain will produce more accurate, natural-sounding localized content.

For example: Marketing, information technology, healthcare, legal services, e-commerce, SaaS products

üåê Web Localization Context
Be cautious of translators who claim expertise in too many unrelated fields, especially if they don't have the years of experience that a broad range of specializations would require. Specialization is a signal of credibility‚Äîthe more domains someone claims, the less likely they are to be truly specialized in any of them.

Indicators to Look For

  • Professional background or work history in the subject area
  • Formal studies or degree in the field
  • Extended experience translating about the subject
  • Does not claim expertise in too many unrelated areas

4. Audience Appropriateness

A technically correct translation can still miss the mark if it doesn't connect with the intended audience. This proficiency area is about register, tone, cultural resonance, and community fluency.

For example: Healthcare patients vs. medical professionals; casual gaming communities; professional B2B software users; subscribers to a lifestyle brand

üåê Web Localization Context
Your website's localized content needs to feel authentic to its audience, which means it must resonate with the cultural and communication norms of that audience. Ask translators whether they are familiar with the culture and communication norms of your target market, and how they adapt tone and register for different audiences online.

Indicators to Look For

  • Membership within or lived experience of the target audience community
  • Professional experience communicating with that specific audience
  • Affinity group memberships relevant to the audience
üíª

5. Technology

Web localization involves specific technologies. A qualified translator should be familiar with the coding languages and tools used to build multilingual websites, and ideally with the specific platforms where the translated content will live.

For example: Programming languages like HTML, JavaScript, and CSS; CMS platforms like WordPress; localization formats like JSON or PO files for translatable strings

üåê Web Localization Context
As you'll learn in the next topic, we do not recommend that CAT (computer-assisted translation) tools be used to translate the majority of web content. Where CAT tools can be effective for translating navigational elements like UI strings, page content and blog posts should be almost be written from scratch so as to resonate with the target audience and perform well in search engines.

Indicators to Look For

  • Professional experience using relevant coding languages and web tools
  • Formal training or certificates in UX design, web development or web localization
  • Familiarity with file formats used in web localization workflows
üìù

6. Specifications

Professional translators work from specifications‚Äîa defined set of requirements that clarify the purpose, audience, tone, scope, and deliverables of a project. A translator who works without specifications introduces unnecessary risk.

For example: Defining the target audience and purpose before starting; agreeing on glossaries and style guides; establishing file formats, timelines, and review processes

üåê Web Localization Context
Web localization specifications should include: the site's target locale, the CMS or platform being used, any SEO keyword requirements in the target language, brand voice guidelines, and expectations around metadata, alt text, and navigational elements. A professional translator will ask for this information before starting.

Indicators to Look For

  • Their marketing materials or intake process references the use of project specifications
  • Before starting work, the translator collaborates with you to establish project requirements
ü§ù

7. Business Practices

Sound business practices signal professionalism, reliability, and ethical conduct. This is especially important for web localization, where your content may include confidential product information, unreleased features, or sensitive customer-facing copy.

For example: Transparent pricing, data confidentiality practices, clear contracts, professional online presence

üåê Web Localization Context
Before sharing content for translation, confirm how the translator handles confidential files and have them sign a non-disclosure agreement (NDAs). As you start your working relationship, observe their communication style, how they escalate issues and whether they do so in a timely fashion, and if they meet deadlines.

Indicators to Look For

  • Has a professional portfolio or website demonstrating expertise in a specific area
  • Holds necessary business registration or tax identification
  • Charges rates that are reasonable and not suspiciously below market value
  • Follows established standards such as ASTM F2575 or ISO 17100
  • Subscribes to a professional code of ethics
  • Has clear methodologies for protecting confidential content

üó∫Ô∏è Putting It All Together

No single proficiency area tells the full story. A strong web localization candidate will typically show solid language proficiency and professional translation experience as a baseline, combined with relevant subject matter expertise and familiarity with web-specific tools and workflows.

Use the checklist as a conversation guide when interviewing or reviewing a translator's portfolio‚Äînot as a rigid scoring sheet. The best translator for your project is the one whose experience and skills align most closely with the specific content, audience, and technical environment of your website.

üí° A Note on Test Translations
Many clients ask translators to complete a short test translation before committing to a project. This is a legitimate practice‚Äîwith one important caveat: professional translators should be compensated for their time. While requesting unpaid work is a practice that many big box translation agencies do, it signals a lack of respect for translators' professional expertise. If you request a test, pay for it, and let the translator know how their work will be used.
