TOPIC 9

Topic 9 Conclusion

Reflect on what you've learned about localization quality assurance, from the LQA sanity checklist to hands-on project work, and what a developer's perspective adds to your understanding of why LQA matters.

🗝️ Key Takeaways

  • LQA (localization quality assurance) is distinct from translation review: it evaluates whether a localized product functions correctly as a complete system, not just whether the text is accurate
  • The five LQA categories of translated content, code integrity, layout and locale formatting, accessibility, and discoverability map to different layers of a localized website and require different types of attention
  • Code integrity issues like broken tags, corrupted placeholders, malformed JSON are among the most damaging LQA errors because they can silently break functionality rather than just looking wrong
  • Layout and formatting problems such as truncated text are often invisible to translators working from a spreadsheet but immediately obvious to users of the localized site
  • Accessibility in a localized site means more than translated visible text: alt attributes, ARIA labels, and language declarations must all be correct for assistive technologies to serve users in the target language
  • Discoverability LQA covers the metadata layer: page titles, meta descriptions, Open Graph tags, and hreflang attributes that search engines and AI systems use to understand and surface localized content
  • The Speedrun LQA Project demonstrated that completeness errors like untranslated strings, missing attribute translations, and incomplete JSON are often more widespread than they first appear, and that systematic review catches things that casual reading misses
  • Context is essential to LQA: reviewing strings in isolation leads to technically correct but contextually wrong judgments; whenever possible, evaluate translations inside the live product or interface
  • Localization decisions are shaped long before translation begins; how strings are stored, how layouts are structured, and how images are handled are engineering decisions that either make localization possible or create obstacles for it
  • Text expansion is a real and frequent LQA concern: a translation can be significantly longer or shorter than the source, and interfaces designed only with the source language in mind often fail to accommodate it gracefully
  • Language professionals who understand the technical layer are better equipped to catch issues that fall outside pure translation review and to communicate effectively with the development teams who need to fix them
  • LQA is most effective when it is treated as an ongoing practice, not a one-time handoff; localized sites change, and quality needs to be maintained across updates

♻️ Reflect on Your Learning

Take a moment to consider these questions. You don't need to write formal responses, but thinking through them will help solidify your understanding.

1

What the Checklist Revealed

Think back to your Speedrun LQA Project and your independent LQA review. Which category of errors was most prevalent in the sites you reviewed? Were there issues that the checklist helped you find that you wouldn't have thought to look for on your own? Which checklist items felt straightforward to apply, and which required more judgment or more technical knowledge than you expected?

2

The Developer's Perspective

Mohit's interview highlighted how engineering decisions made early in a project shape what's possible in a localized version. How does that perspective change how you think about LQA work? Are there issues you encountered in your reviews that were clearly the result of decisions made before translation ever began? How might you raise those kinds of concerns with a development team?

3

LQA as a Professional Skill

Look at the localization work you've done throughout this course. How has your ability to evaluate localized content changed since you started? What would you do differently on a future LQA review? And how would you explain the value of a thorough LQA process to a client or employer who sees it as an optional extra rather than a core part of web localization?
