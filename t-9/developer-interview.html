TOPIC 9

Interview with Developer Mohit Agarwal

Mohit Agarwal, co-founder of ByondLabs and the developer who built LocEssentials' Pragmatic Auto-Translator, sat down with us to talk about what it actually takes to build for the multilingual web, and what goes wrong when localization is treated as an afterthought.

👤 About Our Guest

Mohit Agarwal, Co-Founder & Chief Architect, ByondLabs
Mohit brings over 11 years of experience in product engineering, building intuitive, high-performance solutions at scale. At Helpshift, he architected mobile SDKs (software development kits) deployed across more than 2 billion devices. At Fairmatic, he developed advanced web and mobile applications for driver experience and risk analysis. He specializes in designing scalable, user-friendly client experiences for both mobile and web platforms, and he co-founded ByondLabs, where he leads client-facing engineering with a focus on AI-first software development.

🎙️ The Interview

What do you do? Tell us a bit about your background and work.

I'm a software engineer with more than 12 years of experience. I've built end-user-facing products across Android and iOS mobile apps as well as web apps that have been used by billions of people. About a year and a half ago, I co-founded ByondLabs, where I serve as chief architect for end-user experiences. We provide AI-first software development services, ranging from AI voice agents to AI-enabled applications.

What are the challenges of building for the multilingual web?

A lot of the time, people build user experiences with only one language in mind and then assume that adding translations will be enough to make the product work for everyone else. Translations tend to get treated as a final phase task rather than a core concern, and they often don't receive enough attention. Copy gets handed off to an agency as a spreadsheet, with little to no context about how the strings are used, what part of the app or game they appear in, or what user flow they belong to. The result is often a literal translation that technically says the right words but doesn't fit the product naturally.

We've tried to improve this by including a few sentences of context alongside the source copy, but it's been hit or miss. Sometimes the translator uses that context well; other times we still receive something generic.

RTL is another area people frequently miss. UIs are designed left to right, navigation follows English conventions, and sometimes instructions reference a corner of the screen that is on the opposite side in RTL. That's an inaccurate localization even if the translated text itself is perfectly correct. The same issue comes up with images and icons that contain embedded text. Developers often don't localize those, which leaves the product only partially adapted for that language.

What workflows do you follow when building for the multilingual web?

One basic rule: always set up your project with multilingual support in mind from the start, even if you're only using English for version one. Use the platform's APIs to manage your strings properly from day one. If you don't, you'll eventually have to track down every hardcoded string in your codebase, extract it, refactor, and test again to make sure nothing broke. That's a lot of unnecessary work that's easy to avoid by planning ahead.

Are there any frameworks you follow, such as product checklists?

Android and iOS have excellent native localization support. Their development environments—Android Studio and Xcode—offer solid tooling for managing translations, flagging what's still pending, and handling things like RTL and plural forms. Web platforms have decent support for detecting the user's language, but you generally need additional libraries to get the same range of functionality.

Beyond that, we maintain a product and design checklist so that localization and accessibility stay front of mind throughout the process, not just at launch. A few examples of what's on it:

  • Designs must be provided in both LTR and RTL layouts so that non-obvious layout quirks surface early.
  • A list of new copy and target languages must be submitted before work begins, so nothing gets rushed or forgotten at the end.
What advice do you have for translators and language professionals performing LQA on a multilingual application?

Actually use the application. Go through all the workflows that your translations appear in. Translating from a description alone, or by reading strings in isolation, leaves too much room for assumptions. Having access to the app itself, or at minimum a video walkthrough of it, gives you context that can dramatically improve translation quality. You'll catch things that no amount of notes in a spreadsheet would have revealed.

What about advice for product owners and clients looking to build multilingual websites and apps?

Same idea: see it in action across languages. Don't treat localization as a box to check. Make it a first-class part of your planning. One thing product owners often overlook is text expansion. A translation can be significantly shorter or longer than the source, and if the UI was only designed to accommodate English, a more verbose language might end up truncated with an ellipsis. That's not a good experience for the person reading it.

Do you see many teams building culturally adapted versions of their products from scratch, rather than just translating?

Not so much. But we do encourage people to think about going multilingual from the start of their projects. Even if they'll only be building for one language initially, systems can be future-proofed to avoid unnecessary rework down the line when the organization decides to expand.

Any final thoughts or additional advice you'd like to share?

Translation should never be a checklist item. It needs dedicated product and design thinking from the beginning. Companies that do this well, and Apple is a good example, treat it as something that matters. My best advice: put yourself in the shoes of someone who doesn't know your primary language and try to use your product. Even switching your own phone or app to your second language is revealing. You'll quickly notice how unnatural a surface-level translation can feel.

💡 Key Takeaways

Mohit's perspective from the engineering side reinforces several things you've encountered throughout this course and adds some new angles worth carrying into your LQA work.

What to Keep in Mind
  • Context is everything. Translating strings in isolation leads to technically correct but contextually awkward results. Whenever possible, ask for access to the product you're localizing.
  • Localization is an engineering concern, not just a language one. Decisions made early in development, such as how strings are stored, how layouts are structured, how images are handled, shape what's even possible in a localized version.
  • RTL and text expansion are real issues. Even a perfect translation can break a user experience if the interface wasn't designed to accommodate it.
  • Advocate for being involved early. The best localization outcomes happen when language professionals are part of the conversation before strings are written, not after the product ships.
