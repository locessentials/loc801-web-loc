TOPIC 10

Course Review

You've covered a lot of ground. Before you build your final portfolio project, let's look back at everything you've learned and done across this course — and think about where you go from here.

🗺️ How the Course Was Structured

Loc801 consists of 10 topics that build progressively from foundational concepts to advanced implementation. Each topic combines lectures, hands-on exercises, discussion forums, and practical projects. The course is designed to be completed as part of a course or at your own pace, with an estimated 60-80 hours of work.

The topics were organized into four broad phases, each one building on the last.

📚 Topics 1–2: Foundations
You started by building a conceptual map of the field. The GILT framework, or globalization, internationalization, localization, and translation, gave you a shared vocabulary and helped you understand how each layer of work fits into the bigger picture. Cultural ergonomics pushed you to think about real users, not imagined ones: security questions, interface assumptions, and content choices all reflect cultural contexts that need to be considered intentionally. You also learned to identify website translatables, what actually needs to be localized on a page, and set up a GitHub account, your first step toward building a professional portfolio.
💻 Topics 3–4: Technical Skills
You moved from theory to practice by learning the core web technologies that localization professionals encounter every day. HTML gave you the structural foundation for identifying and working with translatable content. Visual Studio Code became your working environment. CSS taught you to anticipate layout issues caused by text expansion. JavaScript and JSON showed you how modern applications separate translatable content from application logic, a pattern that underlies most professional localization workflows. You completed hands-on GitHub projects in each topic and started building a portfolio of real, deployed work.
🌐 Topics 5–7: Reach & Technology
You developed skills in making multilingual content findable and in working with the people and tools that make localization happen. You learned traditional SEO practices, including keyword research, search intent, meta optimization, alongside the emerging requirements of LLM discoverability. You also built a framework for evaluating human translators against professional competency standards defined by ASTM F43, and learned how to ask agencies the right questions. Finally, you worked directly with a CAT tool, experiencing firsthand both the strengths of segmentation-based workflows and the very real trade-offs they introduce for website copy.
🚀 Topics 8–10: Expand Your Portfolio
You rounded out the course by working in the environments where much real-world web localization takes place. You experimented with WordPress as a CMS for multilingual content. You heard directly from a developer about what localization professionals need to communicate clearly to technical teams — product localization checklists, glossaries of interface elements, and the limitations of machine translation without human review. You practiced Linguistic Quality Assurance (LQA), the structured review process that confirms translated content meets professional standards. And now, in Topic 10, you're putting it all together.

🗝️ What This Course Was Really About

There are facts and skills you picked up along the way, but there are also bigger ideas that run through the whole course. Here are the ones worth holding onto.

Translation and Localization are Complementary Skills

Translation reformulates written ideas from one language to another, navigating the negative spaces where a concept in one language doesn't exist in another. Localization adapts the full experience for a specific audience, including content, design, functionality, and discoverability. These skills reinforce each other: a perfectly translated page that ignores local search behavior may never reach its audience, and a technically optimized page with poor translation will fail to earn their trust. Localization professionals draw on both skill sets and on collaboration with skilled translators to build web experiences that work for real people in real markets.

Technical understanding makes you a better collaborator

You don't need to be a developer. But knowing how HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and JSON work and understanding what happens inside a version control system, a CMS, or even a CAT tool means you can communicate clearly with developers, identify problems before they become expensive, and make better decisions about how projects should be scoped and executed.

Quality has a definition

The ASTM F43 framework distinguishes between Unverified Translation (UVT) and Professionally Verified Translation (PVT). Whether you're evaluating a translator's portfolio, reviewing a CAT tool's output, or checking your own work, the question isn't "does this sound okay?" It's "has a qualified professional reviewed this for accuracy, naturalness, and fitness for purpose?"

Tools shape the work — for better and for worse

CAT tools are genuinely valuable for technical content with high repetition. They can also produce website copy that reads like it was translated sentence by sentence, because it was. WordPress makes multilingual publishing accessible, and it also comes with hidden costs and constraints that affect how you maintain and optimize content over time. Knowing what a tool is good for, and when to set it aside, is a professional skill in itself.

Discoverability is part of localization

A beautifully localized website that nobody finds hasn't been fully localized. SEO in the target language requires its own research; keyword optimization doesn't translate one-to-one across languages. And as more people use AI systems to find information, optimizing for LLM discoverability by being cited as an authoritative source, providing clear and specific answers, and maintaining current content has become part of the professional picture.

Ask better questions

One of the most consistent themes across Topics 6, 7, and 9 was the gap between what vendors, tools, and workflows claim to do and what they actually do in practice. The habit of asking direct, concrete questions about workflows, quality processes, tool use, and deliverable formats and comparing the answers against real examples is one of the most transferable skills you can take from this course.

🔭 Where to Go from Here

Loc801 gives you a broad foundation, but web localization is a field with a lot of depth. Here are some directions worth exploring as you continue building your skills.

Deepen your JavaScript skills

This course only scratched the surface of what JavaScript can do in a localization context. To work effectively on real web localization projects, you'll want to go deeper: learn how JavaScript handles dynamic content, how strings get passed around at runtime, and how libraries like i18next or FormatJS work under the hood. From there, picking up React and TypeScript will open a lot of doors. React is the dominant framework in modern front-end development, and TypeScript adds the kind of type safety that makes large, multilingual codebases much easier to maintain. Many localization engineers are expected to be comfortable in both.

Work with PO and POT files

The Portable Object (.po) and Portable Object Template (.pot) formats are the backbone of the Gettext localization ecosystem, which powers WordPress, Drupal, and a large number of other CMSes and open-source projects. Learning to work with these files and with tools like Poedit will expand the range of projects you can take on significantly.

Practice content and string extraction and learn to quote projects fairly

Knowing how to extract translatable content from a codebase or CMS is a practical skill that gets better with repetition. But extraction is also the foundation of project scoping: word counts, string counts, and file format complexity all feed into how you estimate effort and price a project. Practice on real or sample projects, and compare your estimates against what experienced localizers actually charge.

Implement pluralization, gender, ICU message syntax, and formatting in a real tech stack

Reading about these concepts is useful. Implementing them is where the learning sticks. Pick a tech stack and build something that handles pluralization rules across at least two languages, grammatical gender, ICU message syntax, locale-aware date, number, and currency formatting, and RTL layout support. It doesn't need to be large. It needs to be real enough that you run into actual problems and have to solve them.

Research reading and viewing habits beyond the United States

In Topic 2, we asked you to think about how English-speaking readers tend to scan web pages in an F-shaped pattern. That pattern is not universal. Reading direction, cultural conventions around visual hierarchy, and how people scan pages vary across cultures in ways that affect layout and localization decisions. Do research on reading and viewing habits in the markets you want to work in.

Deepen your CMS knowledge

WordPress is the most widely used CMS in the world, but it's far from the only one. Shopify, Drupal, Contentful, and headless CMS architectures each have their own localization patterns and challenges. Familiarity with more than one platform significantly broadens your professional options.
