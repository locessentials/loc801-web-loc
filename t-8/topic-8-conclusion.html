TOPIC 8

Topic 8 Conclusion

Reflect on what you've learned about content management systems, the realities of localizing inside WordPress, and what the process of scoping and quoting a CMS project revealed about how localization work is actually structured and priced.

🗝️ Key Takeaways

  • A Content Management System (CMS) lets site owners build and manage websites without writing code directly, but for a localizer, it means translatable content is no longer in one clean file; it can be spread across a database, theme files, plugin files, and configuration files
  • WordPress powers roughly 43% of the web and comes in two distinct forms: WordPress.com (hosted, with significant restrictions on the free tier) and WordPress.org (self-hosted, with full control), and knowing which one a client is using shapes what is possible in the localization workflow
  • The "blessings and curses" structure of WordPress reflects a real tension in professional localization: the same ecosystem features that make the platform flexible for site owners also introduce inconsistency and complexity for localizers
  • Theme strings, including button labels, navigation items, and footer copy, are often stored in .po and .pot language files rather than in page content, and some require working directly in .php template files; identifying where all localizable strings live is part of scoping, not an afterthought
  • Loco Translate is the standard free plugin for editing WordPress language files directly from the admin panel; WPML is the most widely used paid solution for managing multilingual content across a full site
  • CAT tool connectors for WordPress exist but are generally not recommended for website copy; as established in Topic 7, segment-by-segment translation damages the tone and flow that makes web content effective
  • Scoping a localization project on a CMS requires going beyond a word count: content lives in multiple locations, engineering is often required, and the structure of the site affects how the work gets priced
  • A well-structured localization quote is itemized, transparent about methodology, and addresses not just translation but also SEO localization, engineering time, and long-term maintenance; a quote that lacks these elements is either incomplete or designed to obscure costs
  • Tiered pricing based on CAT tool match types (full matches, fuzzy matches, new words) can be legitimate but is also commonly used to make projects seem cheaper upfront than they are; understanding what those categories mean lets you evaluate quotes critically
  • Localized pages that have not had their meta titles, meta descriptions, and URL slugs adapted for the target locale are leaving discoverability unrealized; SEO localization is not a bonus feature, it is part of the work
  • A one-time localization quote for a site that publishes ongoing content is almost always the wrong frame; professional localization of a live site requires thinking about retainer structures, maintenance rates, and how future content will be handled
  • The experience of requesting, receiving, and evaluating real vendor quotes builds a practical skill that most localization training does not cover directly; being able to read a quote critically is as valuable as being able to produce one

The CMS as a Localization Environment

What the Quotes Revealed

Localization as a business conversation

This topic asked you to do something different from most localization training: communicate technical scope in professional business language and then evaluate what came back. How did that feel? What did you find difficult about translating technical knowledge into a vendor brief? And how do you think the ability to have that conversation fluently positions you relative to other people entering the localization field?
