TOPIC 8

CMS Project Quote

Research a CMS, scope a small website for localization, request real vendor quotes, and use what you've learned to evaluate them critically.

🎯 Project Overview

Localization costs can be hard to anticipate, especially when a site is hosted on a CMS with its own quirks, plugins, and content architecture. In this project, you'll do the groundwork that makes a real quote possible: pick a CMS, research how its content is structured and localized, scope a small but realistic site, and then request actual quotes from localization vendors.

This project is valuable regardless of your role. Business owners get a concrete sense of what localization costs before accepting or rejecting a service quote. Loc Engineers get hands-on experience with a specific platform's localization workflow. Translators who are asked to quote work build exactly that skill here. And everyone practices something that rarely gets taught directly: converting technical specifications into clear, professional business language, and then turning a vendor's quote back into something you can actually evaluate.

📋 Instructions

1

Choose a CMS

Pick the platform you'll be working with. Good options include Shopify, Wix, Squarespace, WordPress, or any other CMS you're curious about. If you already work with a specific platform professionally, use that one.

2

Research How the CMS Handles Localization

Investigate where localizable content lives on your chosen platform and what the localization process looks like. Your write-up should cover:

  • Where text content is stored and managed (pages, posts, theme strings, metadata, etc.)
  • What plugins, apps, or built-in tools the CMS offers for multilingual support
  • The general workflow for getting content out, translated, and back in
  • Any platform-specific considerations a localization team would need to know

If you can test things out on a free trial or sandbox, do it. Hands-on familiarity now means fewer surprises later, especially once a client has approved a quote and work has begun. That said, a well-researched write-up is what this assignment requires.

3

Scope the Site

Define the site you'll be quoting. Use the following as your baseline scope:

  • 5 standard pages (Home, About, Blog index, Contact, and one more of your choice)
  • 5 blog posts, including their SEO customizations (meta titles, meta descriptions, slugs)
  • 1 header and 1 footer

Translate this scope into business-ready language when writing it up. Vendors don't need to know you're learning web localization; they need to know what work is involved. Describe the site clearly and professionally.

4

Prepare Your Quote Request

Write up a brief, professional request you'll send to vendors. It should describe the site, the scope, the source and target languages, and the CMS. Keep it clear and specific. Vague requests tend to produce vague quotes.

Ask explicitly for an itemized quote. A lump sum with no breakdown makes it impossible to understand what you're paying for, or to compare quotes meaningfully. A vendor who won't itemize their pricing is worth noting.

5

Request at Least Two Quotes

Send your request to at least two different localization vendors. You can use freelance platforms, language service provider directories, or direct outreach. Having more than one quote is the only way to develop a sense of what the market actually looks like.

6

Evaluate the Quotes

Once you have your quotes, analyze them using everything you've learned in the course. Your evaluation should address the questions in the Reflection section below.

🔍 What to Look for in a Quote

Not all quotes are created equal. Some are professional and transparent; others make it difficult to understand what you're getting, or charge for things that don't add value. Here are the key things to examine.

Itemization and Transparency

A trustworthy quote breaks down costs by deliverable: translation, review, engineering work, project management, and so on. A quote that gives you a single total with no explanation is a red flag. It makes it impossible to assess whether the price is fair, to understand if quality checks such as sensitivity readings will be performed, or to negotiate specific line items as needed.

CAT Tool Pricing Tiers

Many vendors who use computer-assisted translation tools will charge different rates for different types of matches: full matches (strings already in their translation memory), fuzzy matches (partial matches), and new words (no prior translation exists). This tiered pricing can be legitimate, but it can also be used to obscure the real cost of a project. Make sure you understand what content they intend to pass through CAT tools, what percentage of that content falls into each category, and whether the rates are reasonable.

Engineering Time

Localizing a CMS-hosted site is rarely just a translation job. Depending on the platform, someone may need to configure multilingual plugins, manage language-specific URLs, handle SEO metadata per locale, or troubleshoot content that doesn't export cleanly. For professional sites, this engineering work is often necessary and should appear somewhere in the quote. If it doesn't, ask whether it's included, or whether the vendor is assuming you'll handle it yourself.

SEO Localization

A localized page that hasn't had its meta title, meta description, and URL slug adapted for the target locale is leaving discoverability on the table. Check whether the quote includes SEO localization explicitly, if long form content will be written outside of a CAT environment, if the quote includes transcreation for callouts and ads. Given what you've learned about keyword research and search intent in Topic 5, you're well positioned to evaluate this.

Long-Term Growth

A site that publishes content regularly will need ongoing localization. Search engines tend to prioritize sites that are updated consistently, so the localization need only grows over time. A vendor who understands this should be able to speak to retainer arrangements, maintenance rates, or how future content would be priced. If a quote treats this as a one-time job with no discussion of what comes next, it may not be coming from someone with experience in sustained localization partnerships.

📝 Reflection

After receiving and reviewing your quotes, write a reflection that addresses the following questions. Be specific: name the vendors (or describe them neutrally if you prefer), point to actual line items, and connect your observations to what you've learned in this course.

1. What did you learn about your CMS?

What was the most important thing you discovered about how your chosen CMS handles localizable content? Were there any surprises in the workflow, the tooling, or the content architecture?

2. How did the quotes compare?

Describe the quotes you received. How did the pricing, structure, and level of detail differ between vendors? Were you able to compare them meaningfully, or did the lack of itemization make that difficult?

3. What signals did you see in the quotes?

Using what you know from the course, what did the quotes reveal about each vendor? Does it seem like the team is truly specialized in website localization, or do they approach it as an act of producing literal translations only?

4. What would you recommend for long-term localization?

Based on your research and the quotes you received, what would you recommend to a business owner looking to localize this site and keep it updated over time? What should they prioritize, watch out for, or ask vendors before signing anything?

✅ Submission Checklist

  • CMS chosen and clearly identified
  • Written overview of how the CMS handles localizable content and the general localization workflow
  • Site scope defined using the baseline provided (5 pages, 5 blog posts with SEO, header, footer)
  • Professional request for an itemized quote written and sent to at least two vendors
  • At least two quotes received and included or summarized in your submission
  • Reflection completed addressing all four questions with specific examples from the quotes
Submission Format

If you're completing this assignment for a course, follow your instructor's submission instructions. If you're working through the course independently, this is a project worth keeping: a well-documented quote comparison is a useful portfolio piece that demonstrates business acumen alongside technical localization knowledge.
